If you've been researching places to relocate in the South, Franklin, Tennessee keeps coming up — and for good reason. Over the past decade, Franklin has quietly become one of the most desirable places to live in the entire country, drawing families from California, professionals from Chicago, and retirees from the Northeast who are ready for something different.
But is the hype real? And is Franklin actually the right fit for you?
This guide covers everything you need to know — the schools, the job market, the neighborhoods, the cost of living, and the things nobody tells you until after you've already moved. By the end, you'll have a clear picture of whether Franklin, TN deserves a spot at the top of your relocation list.

What Kind of City Is Franklin, TN?
Franklin is the county seat of Williamson County, located about 21 miles south of downtown Nashville along I-65. With a population of around 85,000, it sits in a sweet spot that most cities never find: big enough to have world-class amenities, small enough that you still run into people you know at the farmer's market.
It's not a suburb in the sleepy sense. Franklin has its own thriving downtown, its own major employers, its own identity. People who live here will tell you they don't feel like they're living in Nashville's shadow — they feel like they're living somewhere better.
Here's why so many people agree.

1. Williamson County Schools Are Among the Best in the Nation
Ask any parent why they chose Franklin, and schools come up within the first thirty seconds. Williamson County Schools (WCS) consistently rank as the top-performing public school district in Tennessee and routinely appear on national lists of best school districts in the country.
We're not talking about one standout school. The entire district is strong. High schools like Franklin High, Independence High, and Ravenwood High offer Advanced Placement programs, dual enrollment opportunities, competitive athletics, and performing arts programs that rival private schools in larger cities.
For families who have been navigating mediocre or overcrowded school systems elsewhere, Williamson County Schools feel like a revelation. It is one of the single biggest drivers of in-migration to Franklin — and one of the biggest reasons home values here hold strong even when markets elsewhere soften.

2. A Job Market That Doesn't Require a Nashville Commute
A common assumption about Franklin is that everyone commutes to Nashville for work. Some do — and the 20-mile drive up I-65 is relatively manageable compared to commutes in most major metros. But a growing number of Franklin residents never leave Williamson County for work at all.
The Cool Springs corridor, just off I-65, has evolved into a legitimate corporate hub. Major companies with significant operations or headquarters in the Franklin area include:

Nissan North America — North American headquarters
Community Health Systems — one of the largest hospital operators in the country
Mars Petcare — North American headquarters
Tractor Supply Company — national headquarters
Dollar General — national headquarters (nearby Goodlettsville, easy commute)
AllianceBernstein — relocated from New York City

Healthcare, technology, financial services, and logistics are all well-represented. For remote workers, Franklin offers something equally valuable: reliable infrastructure, great coffee shops, and a quality of life that makes working from home feel like a genuine perk rather than an afterthought.

3. Downtown Franklin Is Genuinely Special
A lot of cities claim a charming downtown. Franklin actually has one.
The 16-block historic district along Main Street is one of the best-preserved small-city downtowns in the South. Victorian-era storefronts, brick sidewalks, and a walkable scale give it an authenticity that planned "town centers" in newer suburbs spend millions trying to manufacture and never quite achieve.
What fills those historic buildings matters too. Franklin's downtown is home to acclaimed restaurants, independent boutiques, art galleries, wine bars, and coffee shops that would hold their own in any major city. The Factory at Franklin — a converted stove manufacturing plant — houses over 40 businesses including restaurants, artists' studios, a brewery, and event spaces.
On weekends, Main Street is alive. On weeknights, it's still worth the drive. This is the kind of downtown that makes people say they didn't expect to love where they live as much as they do.

4. Safety That's Reflected in the Data — and in How It Feels
Franklin's crime rate is significantly below both state and national averages. But beyond the statistics, there's something harder to quantify: the feeling of a community that looks out for itself.
This shows up in small ways. Neighbors introduce themselves. Kids play outside. Local Facebook groups are more likely to be organizing a food drive than arguing about crime. It's the kind of environment that's increasingly rare and increasingly sought after — especially for families coming from larger cities where that sense of safety has eroded.
For relocating families with children, this is consistently cited as one of the most meaningful differences in day-to-day quality of life.

5. The Natchez Trace, the Harpeth River, and Serious Outdoor Access
Franklin sits in the rolling hills of Middle Tennessee, and the natural landscape is a genuine asset. The Natchez Trace Parkway — a 444-mile national scenic byway — runs directly through the area, offering some of the most beautiful cycling and driving in the region completely free of commercial traffic.
The Harpeth River winds through Williamson County offering kayaking, canoeing, and fishing. Pinkerton Park, Riverview Park, and the expansive greenway trail system give residents access to nature without needing to drive an hour out of town.
For people relocating from the Mountain West, the terrain won't replace the Rockies — but for people coming from flat, landlocked suburbs or dense urban environments, the hills and green space of Middle Tennessee are a welcome surprise.

6. A Strong Sense of Community and Belonging
Franklin has the kind of community calendar that makes you feel like you moved somewhere with a pulse. The Main Street Festival each spring draws tens of thousands of visitors and feels like a genuine celebration of the city rather than a tourist attraction. Dickens of a Christmas transforms downtown into a Victorian holiday scene each December. Pilgrimage Music & Cultural Festival brings nationally recognized artists to the area each fall.
Beyond events, Franklin has a dense network of civic organizations, faith communities, youth sports leagues, and neighborhood associations that give new residents plenty of on-ramps for building a real social life. This matters more than people realize when relocating — having structured ways to meet people accelerates the transition from "new here" to "this is home."

7. Healthcare Infrastructure That Matches the Demographics
Williamson County's above-average household income and educated population have attracted genuinely excellent healthcare infrastructure. Williamson Medical Center is a full-service regional hospital, and the concentration of healthcare companies in the area means access to specialists and cutting-edge facilities is unusually strong for a city of Franklin's size.
For families with children, the proximity to Vanderbilt University Medical Center and Monroe Carell Jr. Children's Hospital in Nashville (30 minutes away) provides an additional layer of confidence for complex or specialized care.

8. The Cost of Living Relative to Comparable Cities
Let's be honest: Franklin is not cheap. Median home prices in Williamson County are well above Tennessee averages, and above national averages as well. If you're comparing Franklin to Nashville proper, prices are similar or higher depending on the neighborhood.
But the relevant comparison isn't to Nashville. It's to the places most people are actually moving from.
For families relocating from the Bay Area, Los Angeles, Chicago, New York, or the DC suburbs, Franklin's real estate market looks dramatically more affordable. A house that would cost $2.5 million in Palo Alto or $1.8 million in Bethesda might be $750,000–$900,000 in Franklin — with better schools, a lower crime rate, and no state income tax.
Tennessee's lack of a state income tax is significant for high earners. For a household earning $250,000 annually, the absence of state income tax compared to California (13.3% top rate) or New York (10.9%) represents real money — often enough to substantially offset higher mortgage costs.

9. No State Income Tax
Worth its own section. Tennessee does not have a state income tax on wages or salary income. For professionals relocating from high-tax states, this is frequently a top-three reason for choosing Tennessee over other Southern relocation destinations.
Combined with federal tax treatment and relatively moderate property taxes, the overall tax environment in Franklin is favorable — particularly for dual-income professional households and business owners.

10. Proximity to Nashville Without Living in Nashville
Twenty-one miles. That's the distance from downtown Franklin to downtown Nashville. It's close enough to access everything Nashville offers — world-class live music, professional sports (Predators, Titans), an international airport, a growing food and arts scene — without living in it.
For many Franklin residents, Nashville is something they visit intentionally rather than something they navigate daily. That distinction matters. You get the cultural access without the traffic, the noise, and the density. Franklin residents frequently describe this as having the best of both worlds, and it's hard to argue with the logic.
Nashville International Airport is approximately 35–40 minutes from most Franklin neighborhoods, which is manageable for regular business travelers.

11. A Growing but Thoughtfully Managed City
Franklin's city leadership has historically taken a more deliberate approach to growth than many fast-growing Sun Belt cities. There are genuine efforts to preserve the historic core, maintain green space, and manage infrastructure ahead of population growth rather than behind it.
This isn't to say Franklin has no growing pains — traffic on I-65 and 96 during peak hours is real, and housing supply hasn't kept pace with demand. But compared to neighboring cities that have grown without much planning intention, Franklin feels more considered. That translates into a higher quality of life for existing residents and a more stable long-term investment for homebuyers.

12. A Faith Community Unlike Anywhere Else
This one doesn't make every relocation list, but it should. Franklin and Williamson County have an extraordinary concentration of churches across nearly every denomination — and an unusually high percentage of residents who are actively involved in them.
For families of faith, this means arriving somewhere with immediate community infrastructure. For those who aren't particularly religious, it still shapes the culture in meaningful ways: strong volunteerism, community investment, and a general orientation toward family and neighbors that is palpable.

13. The Music Industry Presence
Franklin is home to a significant and growing community of musicians, songwriters, and music industry professionals. While Nashville gets the headlines, a large number of working musicians and industry executives live in Franklin and Brentwood. This gives the area a creative energy that's distinct from a purely corporate suburb.
For those with any connection to the music industry — or who simply appreciate living somewhere with cultural depth — Franklin punches well above its weight.

14. New Development Done Well
The master-planned community of Westhaven is frequently cited as one of the best-executed examples of new urbanism in the South. With its own retail center, community events, golf course, pools, and walkable street grid, it demonstrates what intentional neighborhood design looks like when done right.
Berry Farms, Ladd Park, and newer developments in southern Franklin continue this pattern. For buyers who want new construction without the soul-less feel of a typical subdivision, Franklin has options that are genuinely worth considering.

15. People Who Have Already Made the Move Love It
This is anecdotal but consistent. Talk to people who relocated to Franklin from other parts of the country and the response is almost uniformly positive. The transition stories — from California, from Chicago, from the Northeast — tend to follow the same arc: initial skepticism, faster-than-expected community integration, and a settled conviction that they made the right call.
That's not marketing. That's the pattern that emerges when a place actually delivers on what it promises.

Is Franklin, TN Right for You?
Franklin isn't the right fit for everyone. If you need the density, anonymity, and pace of a major urban core, Franklin will feel too quiet. If budget is the primary constraint, there are more affordable options in Middle Tennessee (Murfreesboro, Spring Hill, Columbia) worth exploring.
But for families prioritizing schools, safety, and community — for professionals who want career opportunity without coastal cost-of-living — for retirees looking for warmth, culture, and genuine quality of life — Franklin, Tennessee belongs on the short list.
The people who move here tend to stay. That says something.
